# Lightweight Ad-Hoc Project Mode v0

Status: current professional product pressure  
Date: 2026-07-15  
Authority posture: professional product interpretation and shaping boundary only; not shared Cadence doctrine authority, implementation truth, schema/API authority, permissions implementation, roadmap activation, package commitment, public copy, or legal policy

## 1. Purpose

This document makes explicit a professional-product pressure that conventional enterprise project management often misses:

> Not every multi-person project needs to feel like a formal department, staffed program, enterprise portfolio object, or permanent organizational structure.

Large organizations routinely form temporary, irregular, cross-functional groups to solve a unique problem. Small businesses, nonprofits, volunteer organizations, committees, task forces, event teams, and informal internal groups do the same.

A team may be:

- temporary;
- cross-functional;
- partly volunteer;
- formed around one unusual problem;
- intentionally low ceremony;
- composed of people who do not normally work together;
- a `ragtag group of super buddies` assembled because they are the right people for this one problem.

Cadence Professional should be capable of supporting that reality without forcing every effort through the heaviest available staffing and governance experience.

## 4. Candidate use cases

### Temporary cross-functional tiger team

A large organization creates a small group to solve an urgent or unusual problem.

Participants may come from:

- IT;
- finance;
- operations;
- legal;
- design;
- customer support;
- executive leadership;
- an external advisor.

They need one shared project space, selected context, decisions, tasks, dependencies, and a closeout path—not a new department.

### Special committee

A nonprofit, board, association, school, or company forms a temporary committee.

Possible needs:

- a charter or purpose;
- participants;
- meeting rhythm;
- proposals;
- selected documents;
- decisions;
- actions;
- visibility to non-members;
- expiration or closeout.

### One-off event or launch team

A group coordinates:

- a conference;
- a fundraiser;
- a retreat;
- a product launch;
- a move;
- a migration;
- an emergency response;
- a one-time customer effort.

The project may have a clear end date and should not create permanent organizational clutter.

### Volunteer and mixed-status group

A project may include:

- employees;
- contractors;
- volunteers;
- board members;
- community members;
- guests;
- external specialists.

The system should not assume one employment or staffing model.

### Small organization without project-management ceremony

A seven-person organization may need shared project truth without requiring:

- complex resource planning;
- timesheets;
- formal role hierarchies;
- portfolio optimization;
- enterprise staffing dashboards.

Professional capability should scale down as well as up.

## 5. Lightweight mode experience

A low-ceremony project mode might begin with only:

- project purpose;
- a small participant set;
- selected visibility;
- lightweight roles where useful;
- tasks or actions;
- dependencies where real;
- shared artifacts;
- dates or milestones where useful;
- decisions;
- one clear closeout condition.

The system should not require unused machinery merely because it exists elsewhere in the product.

Potentially hidden or deferred until needed:

- staffing optimization;
- capability eligibility;
- overtime;
- cost models;
- policy engines;
- formal approval chains;
- complex portfolio displacement analysis;
- permanent org-chart semantics.

## 6. Malleable roles

A temporary professional project may need roles such as:

- organizer;
- steward;
- contributor;
- subject-matter expert;
- reviewer;
- approver;
- sponsor;
- viewer;
- guest;
- facilitator.

These are illustrative, not canonical.

Important properties:

- roles may overlap;
- roles may be project-specific;
- roles may change;
- some people may have visibility without task ownership;
- some people may advise without edit rights;
- some people may review without final approval authority;
- organizational identity may still constrain access;
- one project should not silently rewrite a person's permanent organizational role.

## 7. Progressive governance

The preferred posture is not `no governance`.

It is:

```text
START_WITH_THE_SMALLEST_SUFFICIENT_CONTROL_SURFACE
  -> ADD GOVERNANCE WHEN REAL CONSEQUENCE REQUIRES IT
  -> KEEP ADDED CONTROL EXPLAINABLE
  -> DO NOT MAKE TEMPORARY CEREMONY PERMANENT BY ACCIDENT
```

Triggers that may justify stronger professional controls include:

- sensitive corporate data;
- regulated data;
- spending authority;
- legal approval;
- employment decisions;
- high-risk production changes;
- external commitments;
- audit requirements;
- major cross-project displacement;
- overtime or labor constraints;
- policy-sensitive actions.

A temporary group can remain lightweight while selected actions inside it require stronger gates.

## 8. Shared truth without organizational overreach

The project should preserve:

- what the group is trying to do;
- who is participating;
- what each person can see or affect;
- what is proposed;
- what is decided;
- what is authorized;
- what changed;
- what remains unresolved;
- what closes the project.

It should not assume:

- every participant is staff;
- every participant is assignable to any task;
- every visible item is owned by the viewer;
- every contribution becomes organizational intellectual property by product default;
- every temporary group should become permanent;
- every project needs portfolio optimization.

Legal ownership remains outside this document's authority.

## 9. Closeout matters

Temporary projects should be able to end cleanly.

Closeout pressure includes:

- final decision state;
- completed versus unresolved items;
- retained shared artifacts;
- personal notes that should remain personal;
- participant access removal;
- guest access expiration;
- reusable lessons;
- handoff to a permanent owner when needed;
- archive or retention policy where applicable;
- no silent conversion into a permanent team.

The group may dissolve while the organization retains appropriate project truth.

## 10. Compatibility with personal collaboration

The personal product line has parallel pressure around:

- family projects;
- household coordination;
- friend groups;
- bridal parties;
- hobby groups;
- travel groups;
- care circles;
- collaborative learning;
- temporary event teams.

The professional product should not copy personal relationship semantics blindly.

The shared opportunity is a compatible lightweight participation foundation that can specialize into:

```text
personal/social context
  -> relationship-sensitive, privacy-sensitive, low-ceremony coordination

professional context
  -> organizational trust boundaries and selected governance
```

A future Personal Plus/Premium-like product layer may use advanced shared-social features without inheriting enterprise staffing machinery.

## 11. Relationship to existing expedition orchestration

The expedition scenario remains valid for high-constraint professional orchestration involving:

- staffing;
- qualifications;
- PTO;
- overtime;
- cost;
- multiple projects;
- approvals;
- portfolio consequences.

This document does not weaken that scenario.

It adds another valid professional mode:

```text
EXPEDITION_ORCHESTRATION = high-constraint resource and portfolio coordination
LIGHTWEIGHT_AD_HOC_MODE = low-ceremony temporary collaboration with governance added only when earned
```

Both may share project, task, dependency, schedule, scenario, participant, and lens foundations.

## 12. Product design questions

- What is the minimum data needed to create a useful ad-hoc project in under a minute?
- Can a group begin with names and purpose, then add roles only when needed?
- How should project-scoped roles differ from permanent organizational roles?
- How does selected organizational data enter a temporary project without exposing the whole enterprise domain?
- Which actions require approval even when the project itself is low ceremony?
- How should guests and external experts participate?
- How should temporary groups close out and disappear from active work without losing required history?
- Can one organization mix lightweight projects and heavily governed programs naturally?
- How should the UI avoid making a five-person committee feel like SAP deployment?

## 16. Safe use

Use this document to keep professional Cadence from assuming that all organizational collaboration should be rigid, permanent, heavily staffed, or ceremony-first.

The product should be able to support both a highly constrained enterprise portfolio and a five-person temporary committee assembled to solve one weird problem.

The shared foundation should make both possible without forcing them to feel the same.
